# 9.1 Introduction

Presently, dynamic navigation (DN) is used by many medical specialties. The medical specialties of ophthalmology, otolaryngology, orthopedics, neurosurgery, and surgical oncology all routinely use DN to perform simple and complex procedures with increased accuracy and precision [1, 2]. Outcomes are improved and morbidity decreased as DN allows for a minimally invasive approach [3]. In the field of dentistry, DN historically has been primarily used by oral and maxillofacial and craniofacial surgeons in the hospital. The medical DN systems used were primarily designed for cranially based procedures such as orthognathic, trauma, resective, reconstructive procedures and locating foreign bodies within the head and neck [4, 5]. In the US outpatient setting of the dental office, DN system became available in 2000 with the FDA approval of a system designed to place dental implants. Subsequently, more systems have been approved for this indication.

Approved Indications for DN:  
- Placement of dental implants in adults

Implant-Related Procedures:

Ridge splitting for implant placement  
Root shield or partial root extractions  
Finding and removing loose abutment screws in implant crowns  
- Removing failed implants  
Sinus elevation utilizing osseodensification  
Nerve lateralization and transposition for implant placement

"Off Label" Uses of DN:

- Removal of foreign bodies (i.e., screws and needles)  
Deep bone biopsies  
Harvesting bone grafts

- Removal of tori  
- Extraction of difficult third molars and coronectomy  
- Extraction of supernumerary teeth  
Endodontic access of calcified canals  
Apicoectomies  
Osteotomies

In summary, DN can be used to localize any ridged instrument in complex anatomic locations in real time with high accuracy and precision.

The surgical placement and restoration of dental implants has become an important part of contemporary dental practice. This trend has been accelerated by the increased utilization of three-dimensional (3D) imaging including cone beam computed tomography (CBCT) and intraoral scanning (IOS) in the office-based and academic settings. These digital imaging modalities allow the implant team to perform restoratively driven planning in a collaborative fashion using computer-assisted surgery (CAS).

CAS is defined as the general technique of using preoperative diagnostic imaging with computer-assisted planning tools to facilitate surgical and restorative planning and procedures. There are three forms of CAS:

Dynamic navigation  
Static guidance  
Robotics

Presently the majority of dental implants are placed freehand, without any form of CAS. This can lead to the inaccurate placement of implants. Many of the complications associated with the placement of dental implants can be directly related to inaccurate positioning. These include the following:

Damage to the inferior alveolar nerve  
Floor of mouth hematoma and airway obstruction  
Damage to adjacent roots  
- Sinus infections secondary to inadvertent sinus perforations  
Fractured implants due to off-axis loading  
Peri-implantitis due to food impaction and off-axis loading  
Poor esthetics secondary to thin buccal, labial bone and soft tissue  
- Interproximal bone loss secondary to placing implants close to adjacent teeth and implants

# 9.2 Principles of Dynamic Navigation

# 9.2.1 Fundamentals of DN

The dynamic navigation systems presently available in the United States are a form of CAS that use optical tracking. Light is projected from a light-emitting diode (LED) light source above the patient. The light is projected down to the patient and the surgical field. The light is reflected off tracking arrays (passive patterned arrays) attached to the patient and the surgical instrument being tracked. The reflected light is captured by a pair of stereo cameras above the patient. The DN system then calculates the position of the patient and the instruments relative to the presurgical plan. This is done live or "dynamically." A virtual image is then projected onto a monitor for the surgeon and staff (Fig. 9.1). This virtual reality device allows the surgeon to work dynamically on the patient and execute the planned implant surgery. At any time, the surgeon can change the plan based upon the clinical situation [6].

Image acquisition includes obtaining 3D files, usually a CBCT with a digital imaging and communications in medicine format (.dicom) and an intraoral scan in a stereolithography format (.stl). CBCTs are a volumetric 3D image that capture the soft and hard tissues. Medical fan beam CTs may also be used. The datasets consist of multiple files with various acquisition parameters.

The area of the patient imaged by the CBCT is called the CT volume.

This volume is made up of voxels or three-dimension boxes that are organized in a coordinate system. Each voxel has an X, Y and Z position. Parameters are selected based upon the area being imaged and the procedures to be performed. Examples include large field of view (FOV)  $25\mathrm{cm}$  D x 17 cm H with  $0.3\mathrm{mm}$  resolution to view the entire maxilla, mandible, or both. These might be used by maxillofacial surgeons, orthodontist, and maxillofacial radiologist. Small FOV 6 cm D X 6 cm H might be used for one to three teeth with a higher resolution of  $0.13\mathrm{mm}$ . Typically, these would be used for endodontic procedures and partially edentulous implant cases. There is a wide range of radiation doses from various manufacturers and field of views from  $19~\mu \mathrm{Sv}$  to  $1073~\mu \mathrm{Sv}$ , for example, a range of  $19 - 44~\mu \mathrm{Sv}$  for a small FOV,  $28 - 268~\mu \mathrm{Sv}$  for a medium FOV, and  $68 - 368~\mu \mathrm{Sv}$  for a large FOV [7, 8]. Once the image is acquired, it is stored in its original format .dicom for later processing in planning software. CBCTs provide excellent imaging of osseous structures but have limitations related to the dentition and soft tissue. An important point related to the acquisition of the CBCT that is often overlooked is the separation of soft tissues while taking the image. For dental implant planning purposes, a cotton roll or radiolucent material place between the dentition and the buccal/ labial mucosa will create an air contrast zone. This allows the soft tissue in the region of the free gingival margin to be visualized on the CBCT. If the buccal/labial mucosa is allowed to touch marginal tissues, there will be no contrast, and the marginal soft tissues will not be visualized.

Limitations of CBCT alone:

Limited image resolution  
Creation of streak artifacts from metal of dental restorations  
- Inability to visualize occlusion  
- Inability to visualize details of soft tissue

Intraoral scanners provide a 3D surface image of the patients' dentition and occlusion. These are not volumetric images; IOS are a surface. IOS are considered direct data capturing. Direct data capturing removes some of the inaccuracies associated with indirect data capturing, i.e., taking an elastomeric impression, pouring the model, and then using a laboratory dental scanner. IOS have a high degree of accuracy for single and quadrant impressions. When full arches are scanned, the accuracy decreases [9]. The implant team may wish to obtain IOS of the patient before teeth are extracted. If the occlusion is not going to be changed, these images can be saved for later use for planning of ideal implant position and provisional fabrication.

# 9.2.2 Image Processing, Analysis

Once the images are acquired and stored, they are loaded into treatment planning software. There are numerous software packages available, but some key features should be present related to image processing and analysis:

The ability to import and export generic file formats (.dicom and .stl)  
The ability to superimpose the 3D files  
The ability to perform dual scan DICOM superimposition  
The ability to export the images in a common coordinate system as individual or merged item

The accuracy of the superimposed datasets and their associated occlusion are the focus of treatment planning for surgical implants. The accuracy of these merged dataset using corresponding mesh points can be  $0.15 + / - 0.12\mathrm{mm}$  and for the images themselves  $0.18 + / - 0.13\mathrm{mm}$ . There are few studies describing the accuracy of these merged datasets with occlusal registrations of occlusion using IOS. Ranges for the technique used by most IOS, a buccal scan technique, are as high as  $0.41 - 0.49\mathrm{mm}$  [10]. When these clean .stl images are superimposed on the CBCT data, the combined images allow the implant team to plan with ideal 3D images. Osseous, dental, and soft tissue structures are clearly visible as well as the occlusion (Fig. 9.2).

Advantages of merged CBCT and IOS:

High resolution  
No radiographic streak artifacts  
Ability to visualize occlusion  
Ability to visualize details of soft tissue  
- Ability to visualize osseous structure

# 9.2.4 Planning

Diagnosis, planning, and implant surgical implementation go hand in hand. Your ability to accurately place a dental implant is only as good as your ability to visualize and plan. The image acquisition and processing are critical to the planning process. A generic approach that is not specific to any implant system allows the implant team greater flexibility. The workflow for the planning process should be laid out in an intuitive fashion flowing left to right. Component and tools of the software that are important for planning purposes include the following:

- Ability to utilize "generic" implants  
Ability to utilize "generic" abutments  
- Ability to view from an implant centric view  
- Ability to place "halos" of varying sizes around the virtual implant  
- Implant mirroring and paralleling tools  
- Implant centric measurement grid view

The steps for planning include the following:

Mapping the arch form  
- Mapping important anatomic structures such as the inferior alveolar nerve  
IOS superimposition  
Dual scan registration  
Cleaning the image of artifacts  
Place virtual crowns  
Place virtual implants and abutment  
- Ability to export the plan for fabrication of restorations

The planning of implants should be restoratively driven. This starts with evaluating the occlusion and placing the restorative envelope of the virtual teeth in the proper

occlusal position. This can be done using virtual implants in the surgical software. Another option is to use separate prosthetic software to plan the restorations. The plan is then exported from the prosthetic software and imported as an stl into the surgical planning software. The virtual implants should be properly aligned below the virtual crowns for ideal emergence into the prosthetic space. Virtual implants can be placed in adjacent teeth and contralateral teeth. These can then be used to evaluate angle and depth. The mirroring and paralleling tools can then be used for planning of the implant to be placed.

# 9.2.5 Surgical Simulation

Surgical simulation for educational purposes is an important part of contemporary dental training. DN has been used since its inception for the purpose of simulation of dental implant placement and restorative dentistry with varying degrees of success [11, 12]. In medicine, the learning curve for dynamic navigation is well documented [13]. A recent simulation study comparing implant naive dental students placing dental implants with DN vs. freehand showed a statistically significant improvement using DN. [14] Additionally, the

use of DN allows for the documentation of training. The software in the systems keeps a record of the position of the surgical instrument tip relative to the patient. This is called the "pose" of the instrument tip. This can be visualized for debriefing, troubleshooting, and educational purposes.  $\square$  Figure 9.4 shows an image of the poses of a surgical procedure. Used in this fashion, surgeons could be standardized statistically to document competence. In Block's recent paper, the learning curve of three surgeons was tracked, and they became statistically equivalent and proficient after placing between 10 and 20 implants [15].

Surgical simulation includes the ability to export the surgical/restorative plan to CAD/CAM restorative software programs. This allows the implant team to fabricate provisional restorations before surgery is executed. This is termed "dynamically guided prosthetics." The navigated plan includes needed bone reduction, implant position, occlusal relationships, the vertical relationships, and the final milled hybrid prosthesis. Small vertical guidance pins milled into the prosthesis are used to

place the prosthesis into the proper position. The osteotomies for these pins are cut using the DN system. Lateral non-guided feet are added to improve the accuracy of the dynamically guided prosthesis (Fig. 9.4).

# 9.3 Accuracy and Precision of Dynamic Navigation

Presently the majority of dental implants are placed freehand, without any form of CAS. This can lead to the inaccurate placement of implants. Many of the complications associated with the placement of dental implants can be directly related to inaccurate position. These include the following:

Damage to the inferior alveolar nerve  
Floor of mouth hematoma and airway obstruction  
Damage to adjacent roots  
- Sinus infections secondary to inadvertent sinus perforations  
Fractured implants due to off-axis loading  
Peri-implantitis due to food impaction and off-axis loading  
Poor esthetics secondary to thin buccal bone and soft tissue  
Interproximal bone loss secondary to placing implants to close to adjacent teeth and implants  
- Increased prosthetic complexity and cost

Accuracy is defined as the degree of closeness between a measurement and the measurement's true value (mean deviation). Precision is the degree to which repeated measurements under the same conditions are unchanged (standard deviation from mean deviation) (Fig. 9.5) [16].

The inaccurate placement of dental implants placed freehand has been documented in the dental literature [15, 17, 18]. This has not only been documented in the clinical literature but on models and cadavers as well [19]. Vercruyssen [17] reported mean angular deviations for edentulous mucosal born guides of 2.71 degrees (SD 1.36) vs. freehand of 9.92 (SD 6.01). Block [18] reported mean angular deviations for DN placed implants of 2.97 (SD 2.08) vs. freehand of 6.50 (SD 4.21). Why does this occur? Dentists need to be able to accurately evaluate shapes, contours, proportions, color, symmetry, depth, and distance [20, 21]. One reason may be that human vision is susceptible to stereoscopic parallax error (PE). PE is defined as the perceived shift in an object's position as it is viewed from different angles. When viewing the space available within a defined edentulous area, there is a viewable or perceived space. This is the "viewable restorative space" (VRS). This VRS is always smaller, distal, and buccal from the actual restorative space. In summary, the literature is clear; any form of CAS is statistically more accurate and precise than freehand placement as they overcome the inherent inaccuracy of human vision.

# 9.4 Components of Dynamic Navigation

# 9.4.1 Important Definitions

CBCT volume (CTV): Volume imaged by the CT scanner. It has an X,Y,Z coordinate system.  
Surgical tracking volume (STV): The area imaged and tracked by the DN system. It has an X,Y,Z coordinate system.  
- Registration: Aligning the X,Y,Z coordinates of the surgical tracking volume and the CT volume with each other using the patient tracking array. This can be done using pair point-based (fiducials) or surface matching (fiducial free) routines.  
- Calibration: Measures the geometry of the device being guided relative to the tracking arrays.  
System check: Verifying calibration and registration are accurate by touching a known anatomic point on the patient and visualizing the same point on the DN monitor.

All DN systems have the following components:

Computer: Graphic processor  
Monitor: Visualization for surgeon and staff  
- Cameras: Surgical tracking volume  
Digital-analog interface: Fiducials  
Patient tracking array: Dynamic reference frame  
Instrument array: Patient effectors  
Planning software  
Tracking software  
Surgical handpiece chuck and drill extension

# 9.4.2 Computer

For any form of guided surgery, a computer is necessary. DN is unique as it is a live image and requires a high level of graphic processing. Ideally a system that is dedicated and designed to perform this purpose will be more robust. GPU (graphics processing unit)-based systems similar to "gaming" computers are preferable.

# 9.4.3 Monitor

DN is a form of "image"-guided surgery. The surgeon and staff will be looking at a computer monitor and not the patient during surgery. A large screen that is easily visualized from anywhere in the operative during surgery is ideal.

# 9.4.4 Cameras

The system tracks the patient using stereoscopic cameras. The area being tracked by the two cameras is called the surgical tracking volume (STV). This area is determined by the cameras. The same parameters used for all forms of photography are important:

Focal distance  
Depth of field  
Pixel size

Another important parameter is the distance between the stereo cameras. This is called the baseline distance or surgical baseline. Generally larger baselines produce improved three-dimensionality by increasing the disparity for a given change in depth. This means the system can measure depth with more accuracy. A fixed baseline is used for all medical and dental navigation systems. Determining the ideal components of the camera system is complex but follows a basic formula:

$$
D = \frac {f * b}{d * p s}
$$

$D =$  Depth;  $f =$  focal length;  $b =$  baseline;  $d =$  disparity value;  $ps =$  pixel size

Depth is the distance from the cameras to the surface of the object being imaged (Fig. 9.6). Disparity refers to the difference in a pixel position between two camera images. The manufactures use various hardware to optimize the DN system to establish optimal STV. [22]

# 9.4.6 Patient Tracking Arrays

The patient tracking array (PRA) attaches directly to the patient in a ridged fashion to allow the DN system to track the patient during surgery. There are two types of arrays:

Passive arrays that reflect light projected on them from the cameras above  
Active arrays that actively project light

Figure 9.7 shows a number of patient tracking arrays. For dentate cases, PTAs are attached to the patient's teeth in an area that the dental implant will not be placed.

For edentulous cases, they can be screwed into the patient's skeleton, usually the maxilla or mandible. Tracking arrays should be large enough to allow the

DN tracking cameras to accurately track but not too large to be obstructed by the doctor or the assistant's hands or head. When the arrays cannot be visualized by the stereo cameras of the DN system, tracking is blocked. This is called optical interference (Fig. 9.8). The surgical team must learn to operate without blocking the arrays.

The number of points and the geometry of the tracking arrays is also important. In general, the more points tracked, the more accurate and robust the system will be. When the PTA is registered to the STV and CTV, the virtual construct becomes the dynamic reference frame (DRF). The DAI must be calibrated to the PTA for this to occur. Ideally, all arrays are calibrated prior to surgery. This is done by holding the array within the surgical tracking volume and activating the software. This can be done at any time during surgery should a part come loose, or a new position be desired for the array.

# 9.4.7 Instrument Tracking Array

Instruments also have arrays attached to them. They must be rigidly attached to the instrument being tracked. They have the same attributes as the patient tracking arrays. Ideally, they should be small, light, and ergonomically designed. When they are calibrated, they become the patient effector (PE).

# 9.4.9 Tracking Software

The software used to guide the instrument must allow visualization in six degrees of freedom.

All ridged objects moved in three-dimensional space have their position describe in six degrees of freedom: up/down, pitch, roll, yaw, left/right, and forward and back (Fig. 9.9).

The tracking software must intuitively allow the surgeon to visualize the proper position in one window (Fig. 9.10).

When using the tracking software, the surgeon proceeds systematically:

1. Platform position is acquired; the tip of the drill is positioned in the proper point of entry (forward/back and left/right).  
2. Implant angle is acquired (pitch, roll, yaw).  
3. Depth is executed (up/down), keeping the first four parameters.

![](images/7ccfcca28ad50263172ca7ad4bbf7685250be7f0c30ed36ffe5cbe44dbdf7c80.jpg)

The DN system allows the surgeon to visualize the STV with micron accuracy and thus requires a steady hand. The utilization of finger rests is imperative for optimal control.

# 9.4.10 Surgical Handpiece Chuck and Drill Extension

Various DN systems allow the use of multiple standard slow-speed implant drills. They all require some form of attachment apparatus for the tracking arrays.

All mechanical rotating systems have some inaccuracy. This inaccuracy is call run-out. There are two components to run-out: radial and axial run-out.

It is important to understand this source of error and its clinical effect. For a standard  $13\mathrm{mm}$  drill, the error within the handpiece itself can be up to 400 microns. If a drill extension is used, the error increased up to  $1.5\mathrm{mm}$  (Fig. 9.11). Since the deflection angle increases with the use of a drill extension, they should be avoided when possible. Longer single-piece drills are preferable.

# 9.5.1 Workflow Overview

A dynamic navigation system enables the user to evaluate the images from the CT scan for virtual implant surgical planning. Prior to starting surgery, the case should be planned on the dynamic navigation system itself, or on a planning workstation. A planning workstation could be a personal computer with the planning software of the dynamic navigation system. If the case is not planned on the machine itself, then the case should be imported to the navigation system after it has been planned in computer and prior to instrument calibration.

# 9.6.2 Fiducial Clip Preparations

1. Turn on hot water bath.

2. Obtain cotton forceps or tweezers.

3. Prepare cold water cups and ice for cool down process. Considerations when planning the location of the fiducial clip (with tracker arm and patient tracker attached):

Get as close to the implant area as desired but not in the way of guided drilling.  
- Facial characteristics of the patient (i.e., mouth size, lingual tori, interference of facial features).  
- Does the patient have dental work that could be compromised (i.e., crowns, bridges, and loose or unstable teeth)?

# 9.6.3 Fiducial Clip Impressions

1. Submerge the fiducial clip in a hot water bath at a temperature of  $140 - 160^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$ $(60 - 71^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$  for approximately 3-5 minutes. When the thermoplastic on the fiducial clip is clear, it is ready to be used.  
2. Remove the fiducial clip from the hot water bath using a mechanical device.  
3. Let the fiducial clip cool for approximately 1 minute to reach a surface temperature  $< 40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ $(104^{\circ}\mathrm{F})$ .

4. Have patient sit in a dental chair with their head resting on the headrest.  
5. The feature on the fiducial clip used to mount the tracker arm should be positioned on the buccal side.  
6. Line up the fiducial clip on the three teeth chosen, making sure there is equal distance on lingual and buccal sides. Press straight down until it bottoms out on the plastic surface.  
7. Remove the fiducial clip by pulling straight up just as inserted. Do not rock the fiducial clip.  
8. After an impression of the patient is taken, place the fiducial clip into a cold water bath until the thermoplastic color appears solid white. When placing in cold water, be certain not to touch the sides of the clear thermoplastic against the sides of the container.  
9. Remove the fiducial clip from the cold water bath, and place it back into the patient's mouth to ensure a proper fit. The fiducial clip should fit firmly and should not be easily removed.  
10. Ask the patient if they feel any discomfort on their gums where the fiducial clip is placed.  
11. If patient discomfort is noted when replacing the fiducial clip, then the fiducial clip should be removed and inspected. Look for large areas of flash on the edges near the soft tissues. This should be removed with a lab knife, avoiding any damage to the inside of the clip near where the thermoplastic attaches to the teeth.  
12. Leave the fiducial clip attached to the patient's teeth.  
13. The patient is now ready for a CT scan (  $\boxed{\mathbb{C}}$  Fig. 9.14).

# 9.6.4 Fiducial Clip Placement

A "Snap Test" helps to ensure that the fiducial clip is properly molded and has a snug fit.

1. The fiducial clip is replaced by the surgeon in the area from which it was fabricated, and an audible and tactile "snap" should be noted when it is replaced. The "snap" may not always occur if the patient does not have undercuts. If this is the case, the surgeon should take extra caution to ensure that the fiducial clip cannot be rocked or easily removed.  
2. The surgeon should then grasp the fiducial clip where the tracker arm will be attached and feel for any movement of the clip. It should not rock or move. The surgeon should be able to move the patient's jaw without dislodging the fiducial clip.  
3. If there is movement, then the fabrication should be repeated.

4. If the desired location is found to be unsuitable due to lack of undercuts or short clinical crowns, then a new location must be found, and the fabrication repeated.  
5. If the surgeon desires to increase retention, then a small amount of composite can be temporarily bonded to the buccal occlusal one-third surfaces of the teeth. The surgeon should follow the guidelines of the desired composite material. This can be removed after surgery is completed.  
6. If the fiducial clip is difficult to remove, it should be rolled toward the buccal or labial surface.  
7. If the fiducial clip should lock onto an undercut or bridge, it can be removed by cutting it in half from mesial to distal. The plastic and thermoplastic can then be removed.

# 9.6.5 Dual Arch Considerations

If multiple fiducial clips are being placed in the mouth, extra consideration is required. Best practice is to ensure that the fiducial clips do not touch. If they must touch, make sure to separate the metal fiducials. A CT scan may be taken with both fiducial clips seated in the patient's mouth. Additional considerations are required during the planning stage, the calibration stage, and the navigation stage discussed in the following chapters.

# 9.6.6 Multiple Fiducial Clips on the Same Arch

If multiple fiducial clips are being placed on the same arch in the mouth, ensure they do not touch. A CT scan may be taken with both fiducial clips seated in the patient's mouth.

# 9.7 Edentulous Patient Workflow

An edentulous patient case requires edentulous fiducials (small screws) to be placed in the patient's bone to facilitate registration in the CT scan. The fiducials can be placed through the soft tissue of the patient, through small stab incisions on apical to the mucogingival junction, or directly into the exposed bone by laying a flap. The surgeon must use careful discretion when deciding the location of the edentulous fiducials. The edentulous fiducials are also used in the preoperative process to register with the software prior to surgery. When placing the edentulous fiducials in the mandible, short  $4\mathrm{mm}$  screws should be placed to avoid damage to the inferior alveolar nerve.

# 9.7.1 Location of Edentulous Fiducials

Edentulous fiducial are small temporary bone screws placed in the bone of the patient in the arch into which implants are to be placed. The navigation software looks for this specific geometry.

Screw length is determined by the quality of the bone and the position in the maxilla or mandible. Considerations for length are as follows:

1. The  $4\mathrm{mm}$  screw is generally recommended, especially in the posterior mandible or in areas of dense cortical bone.  
2. In the maxilla or regions of immature, soft grafted bone, longer  $5\mathrm{mm}$  screws may be necessary to ensure stability.

The edentulous fiducials must be placed in the arch that the implants will be placed. If implants are to be placed in both the maxilla and the mandible, then edentulous fiducials must be placed in both arches. The fiducials must be placed in an area that will be exposed using a subperiosteal incision during surgery. They should be placed in the bone that will not be removed during surgery. For instance, if vertical bone reduction is anticipated, the edentulous fiducials must be placed apical to the area of proposed bone reduction. The inferior alveolar nerve and the infraorbital nerve must be considered and avoided when placing fiducials. The edentulous fiducials should not be placed proximal to where the edentulous plate will be placed during surgery.

The edentulous fiducials should be spread out as far as possible around the arch. Place at least one in the anterior region. Place two in the posterior near the second bicuspid teeth on both sides of the arch. One edentulous fiducial should be located close to the implant site. It is important to spread the edentulous fiducials around the mouth to ensure system accuracy. A minimum of four fiducials must be placed in the patient.

# 9.7.3 Dual Scan Technique

Dual scan is the term used when a dental appliance, such as a set of dentures, is superimposed over the patient CT. This can be used to guide implant placement during planning. There is a dual scan button in the dynamic navigation system software which is used to

superimpose the scan of the dental appliance with the CT scan data of the patient.

# 9.7.4 Dual Scan Procedure Overview

1. Apply at least five temporary,  $2\mathrm{mm}$ , sticky fiducials to the dental appliance.  
2. Scan the dental appliance with the fiducials in the CT scanner. Use high resolution (0.3 voxels).  
3. Do not remove the fiducials on the dental appliance. Place the dental appliance into the patient's mouth, and ensure it is seated properly.  
4. Scan the patient with the dental appliance in his or her mouth using the CT scanner.  
5. Upload the patient DICOM to the dynamic navigation system.  
6. Use the dual scan button to superimpose the dental appliance scan over the patient DICOM.

# 9.7.5 Detailed Steps

# 1. Scanning the Appliance

If a dental appliance for both the maxilla and the mandible are being scanned, perform the following procedure for both dental appliances:

- Apply at least five sticky, temporary fiducials directly to the outside of the dental appliance. Ensure they are securely in place and will not shift during scanning or when placed in the patient's mouth.  
- Place the appliance in the CT scanner, and scan the appliance individually. If scanning both a maxillary and mandibular dental appliance, scan them separately, and ensure that each has its own individual set of DICOM files.

# 2. Scanning the Patient

- Place the dental appliance in the patient's mouth, being careful not to disturb the fiducials.  
If both the maxillary and the mandibular appliances are being used, then place both in the

patient's mouth. The patient's dentures should be in occlusion when the scan is taken.

- Scan the patient with the CT scanner.

3. Uploading DICOMs to the dynamic navigation system

- Upload the patient DICOM to the dynamic navigation system using the Add Patient DICOM button on the patient selection screen. Open the case, and ensure that the fiducials for the appliance(s) are visible in the 3D image.  
- Return to the patient selection screen. Locate the dual scan button which is located on the right of the case row, and select it.  
- When the dual scan button is selected, the user is prompted to select the set of DICOMs from the dental appliance.  
- When the correct set of DICOMs are selected, the Dual Scan Parameters window appears.  
- Select the appropriate jaw on the left of the window. The selected jaw highlights in green.  
- Use the sliders on the bottom of the window to set the opacity, and select the Appliance Isosurface HU Threshold. The 3D image may also be manipulated with the orientation buttons or clipped using the lasso tool and scissors.  
- Click OK. The registration process begins.  
The dynamic navigation system is aligning the fiducials in the appliance scan with the fiducials in the patient scan. Wait for the registration to complete before continuing.  
- When the Performing Dual Scan Registration window closes, the case may be opened.  
- Ensure that the dental appliance is placed appropriately in the 3D image. The dental appliance is also visible in some 2D views as an outline.  
If the registration fails, a warning will appear. No superimposition will appear in the patient case. The failure may be because the edentulous fiducials were not in the same position in the patient scan as they were in the appliance scan. It may be necessary to rescan the appliance or the patient (Figs. 9.15 and 9.16).

# 9.7.6 CT Scan for All Patients

When the fiducial clip or edentulous fiducials are ready, a scan of the patient is taken to provide a transform from the CT to the patient in 3D. For proper operation, the field of view (FOV) of the CT image must be large enough to include both the fiducial clip and the surgical site. If multiple fiducial clips are being used, ensure that the CT image includes all of them. A CT FOV size of at least  $6\mathrm{cm}$  diameter  $\times 6\mathrm{cm}$  height is recommended. The CT image voxel should be isotropic and should be no more than  $0.4\mathrm{mm}$ . A CT image voxel between  $0.25\mathrm{mm}$  and  $0.4\mathrm{mm}$  is recommended.

Take a CT scan of the patient, and load the images from the CT scanner on the dynamic navigation system. Patient status is unplanned.

Optimizing Fiducial Detection and Soft Tissue Resolution

When taking a CBCT for the dynamic navigation system, there are additional steps that can be taken during scanning to optimize the quality of the CBCT. These steps will improve the visualization of both the hard and soft tissues, will help alleviate problems with fiducial detection that occur when the patient has a large number of metal restorations, and will overall enhance the surgeon's treatment planning abilities.

- Keep the plane of occlusion that the implant will be placed in parallel to the floor. Use the laser provided by the CBCT scanner to determine this plane of occlusion.  
- Optimize the contrast between the soft tissues. Always put cotton rolls in the buccal vestibule between the teeth and cheeks and between the teeth and the tongue. This will make an air pocket that optimizes contrast on the CBCT, allowing for greater visualization of the soft tissue in the CBCT.  
- Separate the upper teeth from the lower teeth with cotton rolls.  
- Separate the fiducial clip from the opposing jaw using cotton rolls. Teeth of the opposing jaw should not contact the metal fiducials of the fiducial clip. Contact may cause problems with fiducial clip detection.  
- Do not allow metal fiducial to touch any other metal in the mouth.

If the attachment is an E-clip, then the Select Attachments area of the Rx panel remains empty until at least three edentulous fiducials are marked on the Mark Edentulous Fiducials page. Once this occurs, the attachment appears as the active attachment.

(j) Implants: this lists the implants in the currently active jaw. The active implant is highlighted in green. The active implant can be changed by clicking on a non-active button. Implants may be locked (their position is unmovable and the implant size unchangeable). Locked implants are indicated by a lock icon.  
(k) Alignment tool: this tool is only available if there are multiple implants in the jaw. This tool will pivot the active implant about its occlusal end. The occlusal end will not move position. There are two modes. The mode is chosen in a pop-up that appears when using this tool.

1. Mirroring: if the active implant and aligning implant are on opposite sides of the jaw midline (e.g., the active implant is #4 and the aligning implant is #13), then the active implant will mirror the aligning implant across the midline.  
2. Paralleling: If the active implant and aligning implant are on the same side of the jaw midline (e.g., the active implant is #3 and the aligning implant is #5), then the active implant will become parallel to the aligning implant.

# 9.7.21 Choosing the Appropriate Patient Tracker Arm (  $\mathbb{O}$  Fig. 9.29)

Optimal tracking is accomplished when the patient tracking cylinder is parallel with the plane of the navigation assembly (LEDs and cameras). Select the arm that most optimally follows this concept:

1. The universal tracker arm can be used in either arch and in any quadrant. This arm keeps the patient tracking cylinder parallel to the plan of occlusion of the arch on which the fiducial clip is placed.  
2. The right tracker arm has a bend that tilts the patient tracking cylinder toward the opposing arch when placed on the patient's right. It has a green piece to help identify it.  
3. The left tracker arm has a bend that tilts the patient tracking cylinder toward the opposing arch when placed on the patient's left. It has a blue piece to help identify it.

# 9.7.25.2Performing Navigation Surgery in Dentate Patient

To perform guided navigation surgery, one has to understand the navigation screw views first. The top portion shows the drill position in various types of images and viewpoints. The bottom portion shows images from the stereo tracking cameras, overlaid with pertinent tracking information such as detected feature points and an RX panel.

The RX panel is a quick reference for the surgeon containing the following:

The Active Implant Number. The RX panel also allows for the selection of different implants in the case where multiple implants are planned.  
- Implant Specifications. These are the specifications made by the surgeon in the planning stage.  
- Depth Offset Adjustment. Used to adjust the depth of the implant along the long axis of the planned implant. If the implant is subsequently adjusted in the planning workflow, the platform depth offset will reset to 0.  
- Fiducial Clip Number. If multiple fiducial clips were placed in the jaw, then the RX panel allows for the selection of different fiducial clips.  
- Feedback messages, such as a prompt to perform a system check.

# 9.7.25.3 Understanding the 3D Target View

At the top of the 3D view, the drill depth is displayed to tenths of a millimeter. Next to it is the angular deviation of the drill bit axis from the planned implant axis, which is displayed to the tenths of a degree in numeric text. The implant timing is represented by a magenta line. During navigation, the virtual drill is also displayed in the 3D windows. It is displayed concurrently with the actual drill given its position and orientation (Fig. 9.34).

As the drill moves deeper, the depth indicator fills in a clockwise manner from an initial zero depth at 12:00 to a planned depth at 9:00. The planned depth corresponds to the length of the implant and is indicated by a thick green tic mark. The depth indicator is filled in like a circular progress bar, indicating the current drill depth with each tracker update.

At a distance of  $0.5\mathrm{mm}$  before the planned depth (i.e., the implant length), the depth indicator fill changes from green to yellow in color. At any point beyond the planned depth, the depth indicator changes color to red, and an audible alert is sounded (Fig. 9.35).

# Conclusion

The natural progression from analog, 2D imaging, and diagnostics to digital, 3D imaging, and diagnostics has led to an increased understanding of the complex nature of implant surgery and prosthetics. The increased utilization of these digital 3D diagnostic modalities allows the surgical team to see the limitations of freehand surgery. CAS allows the implant team to overcome the limitations of human stereo vision and increase the accuracy and precision of implant placement. Dynamic navigation allows the surgeon to implement digital implant treatment plans in an efficient fashion. This efficiency and flexibility allow the team to utilize CAS on every implant in every patient. High-level statistical evidence clearly illustrates the improved accuracy and precision of CAS over freehand surgery.
